## What Is a UX Audit (Really)?

A UX audit isn't just "looking at your website and giving opinions." It's a systematic evaluation of your digital product against:

- **User needs**: Are you solving the right problems?
- **Business goals**: Is your design driving the outcomes you need?
- **Industry best practices**: Are you following proven patterns?
- **Heuristic principles**: Does your interface follow fundamental usability rules?
- **Technical performance**: Is your site fast, accessible, and functional?

The goal: Find the highest-impact, lowest-effort fixes that will move the needle on your key metrics.

## The Doop UX 48-Hour Audit Methodology

Our methodology combines four evaluation lenses. Each lens reveals different types of problems. Together, they give us a complete picture of where your site is losing money.

### Phase 1: Heuristic Evaluation (Hours 1-8)

**What it is**: Expert review against established usability principles

We evaluate your site against Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics—plus three additional criteria we've found critical in 2026:

1. **Visibility of system status**
2. **Match between system and the real world**
3. **User control and freedom**
4. **Consistency and standards**
5. **Error prevention**
6. **Recognition rather than recall**
7. **Flexibility and efficiency of use**
8. **Aesthetic and minimalist design**
9. **Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors**
10. **Help and documentation**
11. **Mobile-first usability** *(2026 addition)*
12. **AI-assisted interaction expectations** *(2026 addition)*
13. **Privacy and trust signaling** *(2026 addition)*

**How we do it**:
- Two senior UX evaluators independently review the site
- Each evaluator documents every instance where a heuristic is violated
- Evaluators compare findings and prioritize by severity and impact
- Results compiled into a heuristic violation map

**What we find**:
- Navigation confusion points
- Form friction and abandonment triggers
- Mobile usability issues
- Error message problems
- Cognitive overload areas

### Case Study: Prana Ecotherapy

When Prana Ecotherapy came to us, they had a beautiful website that wasn't converting. Their intuition was that the design "wasn't professional enough."

Our heuristic evaluation revealed the real problems:

1. **Visibility of system status**: The booking form had no loading indicator. Users clicked "Book" multiple times, creating duplicate appointments.

2. **User control and freedom**: No way to edit appointment details after submission. Users had to call to make changes.

3. **Recognition rather than recall**: Treatment descriptions required users to remember technical terms from previous pages.

4. **Mobile-first usability**: The booking calendar was unusable on mobile—buttons too small, horizontal scrolling required.

**None of these were "design" issues in the aesthetic sense.** They were usability issues killing conversions.

### Phase 2: Behavioral Data Analysis (Hours 9-20)

**What it is**: Quantitative analysis of how users actually behave on your site

We analyze your existing analytics to find patterns that reveal UX problems.

**Key metrics we examine**:

| Metric | What It Tells Us |
|--------|------------------|
| Bounce rate by page | Where users are immediately leaving |
| Exit rate | Where users are leaving mid-journey |
| Time on page | Whether content is engaging or confusing |
| Scroll depth | How much of your content is actually seen |
| Click patterns | What users are trying to do vs. what you want them to do |
| Conversion funnel drop-off | Exactly where conversions are lost |
| Heatmap data | Where users click, scroll, and get stuck |
| Session recordings | Actual user behavior captured on video |

**Tools we use**:
- Google Analytics 4
- Microsoft Clarity (heatmaps and recordings)
- Hotjar (for additional session insights)
- Your existing analytics stack

### Case Study: Identifying Hidden Friction

A B2B services client had a 12-step contact form that they were proud of. "It qualifies leads really well," they told us.

The behavioral data told a different story:
- 87% drop-off at step 4
- Average completion time: 14 minutes
- Mobile completion rate: 2%

Session recordings revealed users getting stuck on a "budget range" question. They didn't know the answer, couldn't skip it, and abandoned.

**The fix**: Make the question optional, add a "I'm not sure" option, and add context: "This helps us recommend the right solution."

Conversion rate increased 156%.

### Phase 3: User Testing (Hours 21-36)

**What it is**: Watching real users attempt tasks on your site

Nothing—nothing—replaces watching actual users interact with your product.

**Our 2026 user testing approach**:

**Unmoderated Remote Testing** (fast, scalable)
- 5-8 participants matching your target audience
- Task-based scenarios: "Book a consultation," "Find pricing," "Complete a purchase"
- Screen and voice recording
- Completion within 24 hours

**AI-Assisted Analysis** (new for 2026)
- AI tools analyze session recordings for common patterns
- Sentiment analysis of user comments
- Automated identification of confusion points
- Human experts review AI findings and add context

**Task scenarios we test**:
1. **Primary conversion task** (the thing you want most users to do)
2. **Information finding** (can users find what they're looking for?)
3. **Comparison task** (can users compare options?)
4. **Error recovery** (what happens when things go wrong?)
5. **First-time user flow** (can new users get oriented quickly?)

### What User Testing Reveals

Heuristic evaluation finds problems experts can predict. Behavioral data finds problems at scale. User testing finds problems you never saw coming.

Common discoveries:
- Terminology mismatches (you say "Solutions," users look for "Services")
- Hidden functionality (users don't see buttons that are "obvious" to you)
- Mental model mismatches (users expect your site to work like other sites they know)
- Emotional reactions (frustration, confusion, delight at specific moments)

### Case Study: The "Obvious" Button That Nobody Saw

A nonprofit client had a prominent "Donate Now" button in their header—red, bold, top-right corner. Classic best practice.

User testing revealed: 6 out of 8 participants didn't see it. They scrolled through the entire homepage looking for how to donate.

**Why?** The button was in the "banner blind" zone—users had trained themselves to ignore that area from years of seeing ads there.

**The fix**: Added a donation CTA in the main content flow, with social proof: "Join 2,400 donors supporting our mission."

Donations increased 89%.

### Phase 4: Competitive and Comparative Analysis (Hours 37-44)

**What it is**: How your UX compares to alternatives your users might choose

Users don't evaluate your site in isolation. They're comparing you—consciously or unconsciously—to:
- Direct competitors
- Industry leaders (the "Apple of your industry")
- Best-in-class experiences (Amazon, Airbnb, etc.)

**Our analysis framework**:

1. **Direct competitor teardown**: 3-5 key competitors
   - What are they doing well?
   - Where are they failing?
   - What patterns are standard in your industry?

2. **Cross-industry inspiration**: 2-3 best-in-class experiences
   - What do users expect based on their experiences elsewhere?
   - What patterns could you adapt?

3. **Opportunity identification**:
   - Where can you exceed industry standards?
   - Where is everyone failing (and you could win)?

### Phase 5: Synthesis and Prioritization (Hours 45-48)

**What it is**: Turning findings into actionable recommendations

By hour 44, we have hundreds of individual findings. Now we synthesize.

**Our prioritization framework**:

**Impact × Effort Matrix**

| | Low Effort | High Effort |
|---|---|---|
| **High Impact** | DO FIRST | PLAN |
| **Low Impact** | QUICK WIN | DEPRIORITIZE |

**Impact factors**:
- Conversion impact (how many users affected × severity of impact)
- Business alignment (does this support your strategic goals?)
- User pain severity (how much is this hurting users?)

**Effort factors**:
- Development complexity
- Design complexity
- Content requirements
- Technical dependencies

## Frequently Asked Questions About UX Audits

**Q: How long does a UX audit take?**
A: Our standard audit is completed in 48 hours from kickoff to deliverable. This includes heuristic evaluation, data analysis, user testing, and synthesis.

**Q: What do you need from us to conduct an audit?**
A: Analytics access, any existing user research, and a brief on your business goals. The less time you spend preparing, the better—we want to see your site as real users see it.

**Q: Can you audit a site that's not live yet?**
A: Yes, we conduct pre-launch audits to catch issues before they impact real users. This is often the highest-ROI timing.

**Q: Do you audit mobile apps as well as websites?**
A: Yes, our methodology works for web apps, mobile apps, and complex digital products.

**Q: How often should we conduct UX audits?**
A: For most businesses, annually is sufficient. High-growth businesses or those undergoing frequent changes benefit from quarterly audits.

**Q: What's the difference between a UX audit and usability testing?**
A: Usability testing is one component of a UX audit. An audit includes heuristic evaluation, data analysis, competitive analysis, and strategic recommendations—providing a complete picture rather than just user feedback.
